      <p>An <b>intelligent virtual assistant</b> (<b>IVA</b>) or <b>intelligent personal assistant</b> (<b>IPA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Software_agent" title="Software agent">software agent</a> that can perform tasks or services for an individual based on commands or questions. The term "<a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbot</a>" is sometimes used to refer to virtual assistants generally or specifically accessed by <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">online chat</a>. In some cases, online chat programs are exclusively for entertainment purposes. Some virtual assistants are able to interpret human speech and respond via synthesized voices. Users can ask their assistants questions, control home automation devices and media playback via voice, and manage other basic tasks such as email, to-do lists, and calendars with verbal (spoken?) commands.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A similar concept, however with differences, lays under the <a href="/wiki/Dialogue_system" title="Dialogue system">dialogue systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2017, the capabilities and usage of virtual assistants are expanding rapidly, with new products entering the market and a strong emphasis on both email and <a href="/wiki/Voice_user_interface" title="Voice user interface">voice user interfaces</a>. Apple and Google have large installed bases of users on <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a>. Microsoft has a large installed base of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Personal_computers" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal computers">personal computers</a>, smartphones and <a href="/wiki/Smart_speakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart speakers">smart speakers</a>. Amazon has a large install base for smart speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Conversica" title="Conversica">Conversica</a> has over 100 million engagements via its email and sms interface Intelligent Virtual Assistants for business. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span id="Experimental_decades:_1910s.E2.80.941980s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_decades:_1910s—1980s">Experimental decades: 1910s—1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Experimental decades: 1910s—1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Radio Rex was the first voice activated toy released in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was a wooden toy in the shape of a dog that would come out of its house when its name is called. </p>
      <p>In 1952 Bell Labs presented “Audrey”, the Automatic Digit Recognition machine. It occupied a six- foot-high relay rack, consumed substantial power, had streams of cables and exhibited the myriad maintenance problems associated with complex vacuum-tube circuitry. It could recognize the fundamental units of speech, phonemes. It was limited to accurate recognition of digits spoken by designated talkers. It could therefore be used for voice dialing, but in most cases push-button dialing was cheaper and faster, rather than speaking the consecutive digits.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Another early tool which was enabled to perform digital speech recognition was the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Shoebox" title="IBM Shoebox">IBM Shoebox</a> voice-activated calculator, presented to the general public during the <a href="/wiki/1962_Seattle_World%27s_Fair" class="mw-redirect" title="1962 Seattle World's Fair">1962 Seattle World's Fair</a> after its initial market launch in 1961. This early computer, developed almost 20 years before the introduction of the first <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer" title="IBM Personal Computer">IBM Personal Computer</a> in 1981, was able to recognize 16 spoken words and the digits 0 to 9. </p>
      <p>The first natural language processing computer program or the <a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbot</a> <a href="/wiki/ELIZA" title="ELIZA">ELIZA</a> was developed by MIT professor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Weizenbaum" title="Joseph Weizenbaum">Joseph Weizenbaum</a> in the 1960s. It was created to "demonstrate that the communication between man and machine was superficial".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> ELIZA used pattern matching and substitution methodology into scripted responses to simulate conversation, which gave an illusion of understanding on the part of the program. </p>
      <p>Weizenbaum's own secretary reportedly asked Weizenbaum to leave the room so that she and ELIZA could have a real conversation. Weizenbaum was surprised by this, later writing: "I had not realized ... that extremely short exposures to a relatively simple computer program could induce powerful delusional thinking in quite normal people.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>This gave name to the <a href="/wiki/ELIZA_effect" title="ELIZA effect">ELIZA effect</a>, the tendency to unconsciously assume computer behaviors are analogous to human behaviors; that is, anthropomorphisation, a phenomenon present in human interactions with Virtual Assistants. </p>
      <p>The next milestone in the development of voice recognition technology was achieved in the 1970s at the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, Pennsylvania with substantial support of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> and its <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> agency, funded five years of a Speech Understanding Research program, aiming to reach a minimum vocabulary of 1,000 words. Companies and academia including IBM, Carnegie Mellon University (CMU) and Stanford Research Institute took part in the program. </p>
      <p>The result was "Harpy", it mastered about 1000 words, the vocabulary of a three-year-old and it could understand sentences. It could process speech that followed pre-programmed vocabulary, pronunciation, and grammar structures to determine which sequences of words made sense together, and thus reducing speech recognition errors. </p>
      <p>In 1986 Tangora was an upgrade of the Shoebox, it was a voice recognizing typewriter. Named after the world’s fastest typist at the time, it had a vocabulary of 20,000 words and used prediction to decide the most likely result based on what was said in the past. IBM’s approach was based on a <a href="/wiki/Hidden_Markov_model" title="Hidden Markov model">Hidden Markov model</a>, which adds statistics to digital signal processing techniques. The method makes it possible to predict the most likely <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a> to follow a given phoneme. Still each speaker had to individually train the typewriter to recognize his or her voice, and pause between each word. </p> 
      <h3><span id="Birth_of_smart_virtual_assistants:_1990s.E2.80.94Present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_smart_virtual_assistants:_1990s—Present">Birth of smart virtual assistants: 1990s—Present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Birth of smart virtual assistants: 1990s—Present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The 1990s digital speech recognition technology became a feature of the personal computer with <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> and Lemout &amp; Hauspie fighting for customers. Much later the market launch of the first <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> <a href="/wiki/IBM_Simon" title="IBM Simon">IBM Simon</a> in 1994 laid the foundation for smart virtual assistants as we know them today. </p>
      <p>In 1997 Dragon’s <a href="/wiki/Dragon_NaturallySpeaking" title="Dragon NaturallySpeaking">Naturally Speaking</a> software could recognize and transcribe natural human speech without pauses between each word into a document at a rate of 100 words per minute. A version of Naturally Speaking is still available for download and it is still used today, for instance, by many doctors in the US and the UK to document their medical records. </p>
      <p>In 2001 <a href="/wiki/Colloquis" title="Colloquis">Colloquis</a> publicly launched <a href="/wiki/SmarterChild" title="SmarterChild">SmarterChild</a>, on platforms like <a href="/wiki/AIM_(software)" title="AIM (software)">AIM</a> and <a href="/wiki/MSN_Messenger" class="mw-redirect" title="MSN Messenger">MSN Messenger</a>. While entirely text-based SmarterChild was able to play games, check the weather, look up facts, and converse with users to an extent.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The first modern digital virtual assistant installed on a smartphone was <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a>, which was introduced as a feature of the <a href="/wiki/IPhone_4S" title="IPhone 4S">iPhone 4S</a> on 4 October 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> developed Siri following the 2010 acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Siri_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Siri Inc.">Siri Inc.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spin-off</a> of <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>, which is a research institute financed by <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spracherkennung12_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spracherkennung12-10">[10]</a></sup> Its aim was to aid in tasks such as sending a text message, making phone calls, checking the weather or setting up an alarm. Over time, it has developed to provide restaurant recommendations, search the internet, and provide driving directions. </p>
      <p>In November 2014, Amazon announced Alexa alongside the Echo. </p>
      <p>In April 2017 Amazon released a service for building <a href="/wiki/Conversational_interfaces" class="mw-redirect" title="Conversational interfaces">conversational interfaces</a> for any type of virtual assistant or interface. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_interaction">Method of interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Method of interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Virtual assistants work via: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Text, including: <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">online chat</a> (especially in an <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> app or other app), <a href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS Text</a>, <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">e-mail</a> or other text-based communication channel, for example <a href="/wiki/Conversica" title="Conversica">Conversica</a>'s Intelligent Virtual Assistants for business.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Voice, for example with <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Alexa" title="Amazon Alexa">Amazon Alexa</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Echo" title="Amazon Echo">Amazon Echo</a> device, <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a> on an <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Google_Assistant" title="Google Assistant">Google Assistant</a> on Google-enabled/<a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> mobile devices</li> 
       <li>By taking and/or uploading images, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> <a href="/wiki/Bixby_(virtual_assistant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bixby (virtual assistant)">Bixby</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Galaxy_S8" title="Samsung Galaxy S8">Samsung Galaxy S8</a></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Some virtual assistants are accessible via multiple methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Assistant" title="Google Assistant">Google Assistant</a> via chat on the <a href="/wiki/Google_Allo" title="Google Allo">Google Allo</a> and Google Messages app and via voice on <a href="/wiki/Google_Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Google Home">Google Home</a> <a href="/wiki/Smart_speaker" title="Smart speaker">smart speakers</a>. </p>
      <p>Virtual assistants use <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> (NLP) to match user text or voice input to executable commands. Many continually learn using <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> techniques including <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>. Some of these assistants like Google Assistant(which contains <a href="/wiki/Google_Lens" title="Google Lens">Google Lens</a>) and Samsung Bixby also have the added ability to do <a href="/wiki/Image_processing" class="mw-redirect" title="Image processing">image processing</a> to recognize objects in the image to help the users get better results from the clicked images. </p>
      <p>To activate a virtual assistant using the voice, a wake word might be used. This is a word or groups of words such as "Hey Siri", "OK Google" or "Hey Google", "Alexa", and "Hey Microsoft".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As virtual assistants become more popular, there are increasing legal risks involved.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>815</span></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devices_and_objects_where_found">Devices and objects where found</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Devices and objects where found">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Virtual assistants may be integrated into many types of platforms or, like <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Alexa" title="Amazon Alexa">Amazon Alexa</a>, across several of them: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Into devices like <a href="/wiki/Smart_speakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart speakers">smart speakers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Echo" title="Amazon Echo">Amazon Echo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Google Home">Google Home</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_HomePod" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple HomePod">Apple HomePod</a></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> apps on both smartphones and via the Web, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>'s <a href="/wiki/M_(virtual_assistant)" title="M (virtual assistant)">M (virtual assistant)</a> on both <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Messenger" title="Facebook Messenger">Facebook Messenger</a> apps or via the Web</li> 
       <li>Built into a mobile operating system (OS), as are <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a> on <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> devices and BlackBerry Assistant on <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry_10" title="BlackBerry 10">BlackBerry 10</a> devices, or into a desktop OS such as <a href="/wiki/Cortana_(software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cortana (software)">Cortana</a> on <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> OS</li> 
       <li>Built into a smartphone independent of the OS, as is <a href="/wiki/Bixby_(virtual_assistant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bixby (virtual assistant)">Bixby</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Galaxy_S8" title="Samsung Galaxy S8">Samsung Galaxy S8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samsung_Galaxy_Note_8" title="Samsung Galaxy Note 8">Note 8</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Within instant messaging platforms, assistants from specific organizations, such as <a href="/wiki/Aeromexico" class="mw-redirect" title="Aeromexico">Aeromexico</a>'s Aerobot on Facebook Messenger or Wechat Secretary on <a href="/wiki/WeChat" title="WeChat">WeChat</a></li> 
       <li>Within mobile apps from specific companies and other organizations, such as Dom from <a href="/wiki/Domino%27s_Pizza" title="Domino's Pizza">Domino's Pizza</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In appliances,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> cars,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wearable_computer" title="Wearable computer">wearable technology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Previous generations of virtual assistants often worked on websites, such as <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Airlines" title="Alaska Airlines">Alaska Airlines</a>' Ask Jenn,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> or on <a href="/wiki/Interactive_voice_response" title="Interactive voice response">interactive voice response</a> (IVR) systems such as <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>' IVR by <a href="/wiki/Nuance_Communications" title="Nuance Communications">Nuance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Services">Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Virtual assistants can provide a wide variety of services. These include:<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Provide information such as weather, facts from e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> or <a href="/wiki/IMDb" title="IMDb">IMDb</a>, set an alarm, make to-do lists and shopping lists</li> 
       <li>Play music from streaming services such as <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pandora_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="Pandora Radio">Pandora</a>; play radio stations; read <a href="/wiki/Audiobooks" class="mw-redirect" title="Audiobooks">audiobooks</a></li> 
       <li>Play videos, TV shows or movies on televisions, streaming from e.g. <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Conversational_commerce" title="Conversational commerce">Conversational commerce</a> (see below)</li> 
       <li>Assist public interactions with government (see <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_in_government" title="Artificial intelligence in government">Artificial intelligence in government</a>)</li> 
       <li>Complement and/or replace customer service by humans.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> One report estimated that an automated online assistant produced a 30% decrease in the work-load for a human-provided <a href="/wiki/Call_centre" title="Call centre">call centre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversational_commerce">Conversational commerce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conversational commerce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conversational_commerce" title="Conversational commerce">Conversational commerce</a>

      <p>Conversational commerce is <a href="/wiki/E-commerce" title="E-commerce">e-commerce</a> via various means of messaging, including via voice assistants<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but also <a href="/wiki/Live_chat" class="mw-redirect" title="Live chat">live chat</a> on e-commerce <a href="/wiki/Web_sites" class="mw-redirect" title="Web sites">Web sites</a>, live chat on messaging apps such as <a href="/wiki/WeChat" title="WeChat">WeChat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Messenger" title="Facebook Messenger">Facebook Messenger</a> and <a href="/wiki/WhatsApp" title="WhatsApp">WhatsApp</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chatbots" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatbots">chatbots</a> on messaging apps or Web sites. </p>

      <p>Virtual Assistant can work with customer support team of a business to provide <a href="/wiki/24/7_service" title="24/7 service">24x7</a> support to customers. It provides quick responses, which enhances a customer's experience. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Google_Assistant">Google Assistant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Google Assistant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Google Assistant does not store the users' data without their permission. To store the audio, the user can go to Voice &amp; Audio Activity (VAA) and turn on this feature. Audio files are sent to cloud and used by Google to improve the performance of Google Assistant, but only if the VAA feature is turned on.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Amazon.27s_Alexa"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Amazon's_Alexa">Amazon's Alexa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Amazon's Alexa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Amazon’s Virtual Assistant Alexa only listens to conversations when its wake word (like Alexa, Amazon, Echo) is used. It starts recording the conversation after the call of a wake word. It stops listening after 8 seconds of silence. It sends the recorded conversation to the cloud. It is possible to delete the recording from the cloud by visiting ‘Alexa Privacy’ in ‘Alexa’. There is a feature to stop Alexa from listening to your conversations using ‘mute’ feature of Alexa. After muting the device, it cannot listen even if the wake words (like Alexa) were used.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Apple.27s_Siri"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Apple's_Siri">Apple's Siri</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Apple's Siri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Apple does not record audio to improve Siri, it uses transcripts instead. It only sends data which is important for analysis, for instance, if the user asks Siri to read their message, it won’t send the message to the cloud, the machine will directly read the message without server’s interference. Users can opt-out anytime if they don’t want Siri to send the transcripts in the cloud.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presumed_and_observed_interest_for_the_consumer">Presumed and observed interest for the consumer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Presumed and observed interest for the consumer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presumed_added_value_as_allowing_a_new_way_of_interactions">Presumed added value as allowing a new way of interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Presumed added value as allowing a new way of interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Added value of the Virtual Assistants can come among others from the following&nbsp;: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Voice communication can sometimes represent the optimal <a href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Human-computer interaction">man-machine communication</a>&nbsp;:</li>
      </ul> 
      <ol>
       <li>It is convenient: there are some sectors where voice is the only way of possible communication, and more generally, it allows to free-up both hands and vision potentially for doing another activity in parallel, or helps also disabled people.</li> 
       <li>It is faster: Voice is more efficient than writing on a keyboard: we can speak up to 200 <a href="/wiki/Words_per_minute" title="Words per minute">words per minute</a> opposed to 60 in case of writing on a keyboard. It is also more natural thus requiring less effort (reading a text however can reach 700 words per minute).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
      </ol> 
      <ul>
       <li>Virtual Assistants save a lot of time by automation: they can take appointments, or read the news while the consumer does something else. It is also possible to ask the Virtual Assistant to schedule meetings, hence helping to organize time. The designers of new digital schedulers explained the ambition they had that these calendars schedule lives to make the consumer use his time more efficiently, through machine learning processes, and complete organization of work time and free time. As an example when the consumer expresses the desire of scheduling a break, the VA will schedule it at an optimal moment for this purpose (for example at a time of the week where they are less productive), with the additional long term objective of being able to schedule and organize the free time of the consumer, to assure them optimal work efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perceived_interest">Perceived interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Perceived interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Study_results.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Study_results.jpg/220px-Study_results.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Study_results.jpg/330px-Study_results.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Study_results.jpg/440px-Study_results.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2205" data-file-height="1240"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Study_results.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Graphical sum up of the study capturing reasons of interest of Virtual assistants for consumers
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <ul>
       <li>According to a recent study (2019), the two reasons for using Virtual Assistants for consumers are perceived usefulness and perceived enjoyment. The first result of this study is that both perceived usefulness and perceived enjoyment have an equivalent very strong influence for the consumer willingness to use a Virtual Assistant.</li> 
       <li>The second result of this study is that&nbsp;:</li>
      </ul> 
      <ol>
       <li>Provided content quality has a very strong influence on perceived usefulness and a strong influence on perceived enjoyment.</li> 
       <li>Visual attractiveness has a very strong influence on perceived enjoyment.</li> 
       <li>Automation has a strong influence on perceived usefulness.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
      </ol> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_Intelligence_controversies">Artificial Intelligence controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Artificial Intelligence controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <ul>
       <li>Virtual Assistants spur the <a href="/wiki/Filter_bubble" title="Filter bubble">filter bubble</a>: As for <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>, Virtual Assistants’ algorithms are trained to show pertinent data and discard others based on previous activities of the consumer: The pertinent data is the one which will interest or please the consumer. As a result, they become isolated from data that disagrees with their viewpoints, effectively isolating them into their own intellectual bubble, and reinforcing their opinions. This phenomena was known to reinforce <a href="/wiki/Fake_news" title="Fake news">fake news</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echo_chamber_(media)" title="Echo chamber (media)">echo chambers.</a><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Virtual Assistants are also sometimes criticized for being overrated. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Casilli" title="Antonio Casilli">A. Casilli</a> points out that the AI of Virtual Assistants are neither intelligent nor artificial for two reasons&nbsp;:</li>
      </ul> 
      <ol>
       <li>Not intelligent because all they do is being the assistant of the human, and only by doing tasks that a human could do easily, and in a very limited specter of actions: find, class, and present information, offers or documents. Also, Virtual Assistants are neither able to make decisions on their own nor to anticipate things.</li> 
       <li>And not artificial because they would be impossible without human labelization through <a href="/wiki/Microwork" title="Microwork">micro working</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Casilli,_Antonio_A._2019_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casilli,_Antonio_A._2019-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
      </ol> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics_implications">Ethics implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ethics implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 2019 <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Casilli" title="Antonio Casilli">Antonio A. Casilli</a>, a French <a href="/wiki/Sociologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociologist">sociologist</a>, criticized artificial intelligence and virtual assistants in particular in the following way: </p>
      <p>At a first level the fact that the consumer provides free data for the training and improvement of the virtual assistant, often without knowing it, is ethically disturbing. </p>
      <p>But at a second level, it might be even more ethically disturbing to know how these <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">AIs</a> are trained with this data. </p>
      <p>This <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> is trained via <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial neural networks">neural networks</a>, which require a huge amount of <a href="/wiki/Labeled_data" title="Labeled data">labelled data.</a> However, this data needs to be labelled through a human process, which explains the rise of <a href="/wiki/Microwork" title="Microwork">microwork</a> in the last decade. That is, remotely using some people worldwide doing some repetitive and very simple tasks for a few cents, such as listening to Virtual Assistant speech data, and writing down what was said. Microwork has been criticized for the job insecurity it causes, and for the total lack of regulation: The average salary was 1,38 <abbr>dollar/hour</abbr> in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and it provides neither healthcare nor retirement benefits, <a href="/wiki/Sick_pay" class="mw-redirect" title="Sick pay">sick pay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a>. Hence, Virtual Assistants and their designers are controversial for spurring job insecurity, and the AIs they propose are still human in the way that they would be impossible without the microwork of millions of human workers.<sup id="cite_ref-Casilli,_Antonio_A._2019_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casilli,_Antonio_A._2019-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Privacy concerns are raised by the fact that voice commands are available to the providers of virtual assistants in unencrypted form, and can thus be shared with third parties and be processed in an unauthorized or unexpected manner.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_2019_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune_2019-38">[38]</a></sup> Additionally to the linguistic content of recorded speech, a user’s manner of expression and voice characteristics can implicitly contain information about his or her biometric identity, personality traits, body shape, physical and mental health condition, sex, gender, moods and emotions, socioeconomic status and geographical origin.<sup id="cite_ref-KrögerLutz2020_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KrögerLutz2020-39">[39]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developer_platforms">Developer platforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Developer platforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Notable developer platforms for virtual assistants include: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Amazon_Lex" title="Amazon Lex">Amazon Lex</a> was opened to developers in April 2017. It involves <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_understanding" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural language understanding">natural language understanding</a> technology combined with automatic speech recognition and had been introduced in November 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Google provides the <a href="/wiki/Actions_on_Google" title="Actions on Google">Actions on Google</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dialogflow" title="Dialogflow">Dialogflow</a> platforms for developers to create "Actions" for Google Assistant<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Apple provides SiriKit for developers to create extensions for <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Watson_(computer)" title="Watson (computer)">Watson</a>, while sometimes spoken of as a virtual assistant is in fact an entire <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> platform and community powering some virtual assistants, <a href="/wiki/Chatbots" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatbots">chatbots</a>. and many other types of solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
      </ul>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_relevance">Economic relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Economic relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="For_individuals">For individuals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: For individuals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Digital experiences enabled by virtual assistants are considered to be among the major recent technological advances and most promising consumer trends. Experts claim that digital experiences will achieve a status-weight comparable to ‘real’ experiences, if not become more sought-after and prized.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The trend is verified by a high number of frequent users and the substantial growth of worldwide user numbers of virtual digital assistants. In mid-2017, the number of frequent users of digital virtual assistants is estimated to be around 1 bn worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In addition, it can be observed that virtual digital assistant technology is no longer restricted to smartphone applications, but present across many industry sectors (incl. <a href="/wiki/Automotive" class="mw-redirect" title="Automotive">automotive</a>, telecommunications, <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retail</a>, healthcare and education).<sup id="cite_ref-wordpress1_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordpress1-49">[49]</a></sup> In response to the significant R&amp;D expenses of firms across all sectors and an increasing implementation of mobile devices, the market for speech recognition technology is predicted to grow at a <a href="/wiki/CAGR" class="mw-redirect" title="CAGR">CAGR</a> of 34.9% globally over the period of 2016 to 2024 and thereby surpass a global market size of US$7.5 billion by 2024.<sup id="cite_ref-wordpress1_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordpress1-49">[49]</a></sup> According to an <a href="/wiki/Ovum_Ltd." title="Ovum Ltd.">Ovum</a> study, the "native digital assistant installed base" is projected to exceed the world's population by 2021, with 7.5 billion active voice AI–capable devices.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-50">[50]</a></sup> According to Ovum, by that time "Google Assistant will dominate the voice AI–capable device market with 23.3% market share, followed by Samsung's Bixby (14.5%), Apple's Siri (13.1%), Amazon's Alexa (3.9%), and Microsoft's Cortana (2.3%)."<sup id="cite_ref-:2_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-50">[50]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Taking into consideration the regional distribution of market leaders, North American companies (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nuance_Communications" title="Nuance Communications">Nuance Communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/EGain" title="EGain">eGain</a>) are expected to dominate the industry over the next years, due to the significant impact of BYOD (<a href="/wiki/Bring_Your_Own_Device" class="mw-redirect" title="Bring Your Own Device">Bring Your Own Device</a>) and enterprise mobility business models. Furthermore, the increasing demand for smartphone-assisted platforms are expected to further boost the North American Intelligent Virtual Assistant (IVA) industry growth. Despite its smaller size in comparison to the North American market, the intelligent virtual assistant industry from the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> region, with its main players located in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is predicted to grow at an annual growth rate of 40% (above global average) over the 2016–2024 period.<sup id="cite_ref-wordpress1_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordpress1-49">[49]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_opportunity_for_enterprises">Economic opportunity for enterprises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Economic opportunity for enterprises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Virtual assistants should not be only seen as a gadget for individuals, as they could have a real economic utility for enterprises. As an example, a virtual assistant can take the role of an always available assistant with an encyclopedic knowledge. And which can organize meetings, check inventories, verify informations. Virtual Assistants are all the more important that their integration in small and middle-sized enterprises often consists in an easy first step through the more global adaptation and use of <a href="/wiki/Internet_of_things" title="Internet of things">Internet of Things (IoT)</a>. Indeed IoT technologies are first perceived by small and medium-sized enterprises as technologies of critical importance, but too complicated, risky or costly to be used.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In May 2018, researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, published a paper that showed audio commands undetectable for the human ear could be directly embedded into music or spoken text, thereby manipulating virtual assistants into performing certain actions without the user taking note of it.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-52">[52]</a></sup> The researchers made small changes to audio files, which cancelled out the sound patterns that speech recognition systems are meant to detect. These were replaced with sounds that would be interpreted differently by the system and command it to dial phone numbers, open websites or even transfer money.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-52">[52]</a></sup> The possibility of this has been known since 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-52">[52]</a></sup> and affects devices from <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_(company)" title="Amazon (company)">Amazon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In addition to unintentional actions and voice recording, another security and privacy risk associated with intelligent virtual assistants is malicious voice commands: An attacker who impersonates a user and issues malicious voice commands to, for example, unlock a smart door to gain unauthorized entry to a home or garage or order items online without the user's knowledge. Although some IVAs provide a voice-training feature to prevent such impersonation, it can be difficult for the system to distinguish between similar voices. Thus, a malicious person who is able to access an IVA-enabled device might be able to fool the system into thinking that they are the real owner and carry out criminal or mischievous acts.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> </p>
