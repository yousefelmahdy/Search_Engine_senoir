    <h2 id="why-is-this-page-called-is-it-any-good">Why is this page called 'Is it any good?'</h2> 
    <p>When people hear about a new product they often ask if it is any good. <a href="https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=3067434">A Hacker News user once wrote</a>: "Starting immediately, all raganwald projects will have a 'Is it any good?' section in the readme, and the answer shall be 'yes'." We were inspired and added that section to the <a href="https://gitlab.com/gitlab-org/gitlab-foss/blob/master/README.md#is-it-any-good">GitLab readme</a>.</p> 
    <p>Don't only take our word for it! GitLab is the only comprehensive <a href="/solutions/devops-platform/">DevOps platform</a> being delivered as a single application, and our unique breadth in DevOps has enabled us to enjoy similarly <a href="/analysts/">broad coverage across analysts’ categories and reports</a>.</p> 
    <p>The GitLab solution is the result of collaboration between <a href="http://contributors.gitlab.com/">thousands of community contributors</a>, based on feedback from the over 100,000 organizations using GitLab to power their DevOps transformations. The GitLab community contributes code, documentation, translation, design, and product ideas based on their real world challenges, making GitLab more useful and valuable every day.</p> 
    <p>Because of the community behind GitLab, in many cases we see rapid adoption through better developer experience and collaboration. We've <a href="https://twitter.com/gitlab/likes">liked thousands of supportive tweets</a> from <a href="https://twitter.com/gitlab/">our 100,000+ followers on Twitter</a>.</p> 
    <p>This page shares more of the many acknowledgements GitLab has enjoyed.</p>

    <h2 id="gitlab-is-the-most-used-devops-tool">GitLab is the most used DevOps tool</h2> 
    <p>GitLab now has more than <strong>30 million estimated registered users</strong>!</p> 
    <p>This is an estimate, since not all self-managed instances of GitLab report back their user count. How did we arrive at 30 million? We currently use the <a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/development/usage_ping/">Usage Ping</a> to estimate the total registered users. The Usage Ping is an optional feature for Self-Managed instances. To date, ~68% of paid Self-Managed instances have shared their usage data with GitLab.</p> 
    <p><code>Estimated Lifetime Total Registered Users (TRU)</code> = <code>Lifetime Estimated Self-Managed Free Users</code> + <code>Lifetime Estimated Self-Managed Paid Users</code> + <code>Lifetime Self-Managed EDU Users</code> + <code>Lifetime GitLab.com SaaS Users</code></p> 
    <ul> 
     <li><code>Lifetime Estimated Self-Managed Free Users</code> - this is estimated using the opt-in rate for Paid Accounts.</li> 
     <li><code>Lifetime Estimated Self-Managed Paid Users</code> - this is estimated using the opt-in rate for Paid Accounts.</li> 
     <li><code>Lifetime Self-Managed EDU Users</code> - this is known and is the lifetime highest user count per EDU account.</li> 
     <li><code>Lifetime GitLab.com SaaS Users</code> - this is known and is the TRU of GitLab.com.</li> 
    </ul> 
    <p>In using this calculation we are making an assumption that paid installations have the usage ping enabled at a similar rate as unpaid installations. Our <a href="/handbook/customer-success/tam/usage-ping-faq/">Customer Success group asks customers to turn usage ping on</a> in order to better help our customers.</p> 
    <p>Here's the calculation with actual numbers:</p> 
    <ul> 
     <li><code>16.21mm</code> * (1 / <code>0.68</code>) + <code>0.65mm</code> * (1 / <code>0.68</code>) + <code>0.20mm</code> + <code>6.20mm</code> = <code>31.19 million users</code> for July 2020</li> 
    </ul> 
    <p>We have been hesitant to release these numbers as they are not exact. It isn’t possible for us to have exact numbers because we are an open source project that people can run without registering. Previously, we reported the number of GitLab installations, however, this lead people to mistaken the number of installations as our number of users.</p> 
    <p>All of these numbers are based on registered users since there are no publicly known numbers for active users reported by our competitors. Registrations on self-managed GitLab installations that were online but are no longer online are disregarded.</p> 
    <p>GitHub, which has <a href="https://github.com/search?q=type:user&amp;type=Users">~50 million users</a>, is closed source software that is more frequently used to produce open source software. GitLab is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-core_model">open core</a> software that is more commonly used to write closed source software. Most software written is closed source. Typically (not always) closed source is a core part of a job, while contributing to open source typically (not always) is a smaller part of a job or something that happens outside work. The statement that GitLab has higher usage incorporates the expectation that a registered user in GitLab uses the application more frequently than a typical registered user in GitHub.</p>

    <h2 id="gitlab-has-23-market-share-in-the-self-managed-git-market">GitLab has 2/3 market share in the self-managed Git market</h2> 
    <p>With more than 100,000 organizations self-hosting GitLab, we have the largest share of companies who choose to host their own code. We’re estimated to have two-thirds of this single-tenant market.</p> 
    <p>When <a href="http://blog.bitrise.io/2017/01/27/state-of-app-development-in-2016.html#self-managed">Bitrise surveyed</a> ten thousand developers who build apps regularly on their platform, they found that 67 percent of self-managed apps prefer GitLab’s on-premise solution.</p> 
    <p><a href="http://blog.bitrise.io/2017/01/27/state-of-app-development-in-2016.html#self-managed"><img src="/images/blogimages/bitrise-self-hosted-chart.png" alt="Image via Bitrise blog" class="medium"></a><br></p> 
    <p>Similarly, in their survey of roughly one thousand development teams, <a href="https://www.buddybuild.com/blog/source-code-hosting#selfhosted">BuddyBuild found</a> that 79% of mobile developers who host their own code have chosen GitLab:</p> 
    <p><a href="https://www.buddybuild.com/blog/source-code-hosting#selfhosted"><img src="/images/blogimages/buddybuild-self-hosted-chart.png" alt="Image via buddybuild blog" class="medium"></a><br></p> 
    <p>In their articles, both Bitrise and BuddyBuild note that few organizations use self-managed instances. We think there is a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_bias">selection effect</a>, because both of them are SaaS-only offerings.</p> 
    <p>Based on our experience, the vast majority of enterprises (<a href="/handbook/sales/field-operations/gtm-resources/#segmentation">organizations with 2,000+ employees</a>) self-host their source code server, frequently on a cloud service like AWS or GCP, instead of using a SaaS service.</p> 
    <p>Another assumption is that Git is the most popular version control technology for the enterprise. Certainly not every enterprise has completely switched yet, but it does seem that Git is <a href="https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?q=git,svn,perforce,mercurial,tfs">almost 10 times larger than SVN and Mercurial</a>.</p> 
    <p>Per the dominance of Git, the SCM market is lacking analysis that is as complete, independent, and directly relevant to the question of market share as GitLab would prefer, but with these qualifications and multiple sources, we find the data and discussions sufficient to share. GitLab has encouraged additional, independent analysts to address the SCM market and will continue to do so.</p>
