      <p><i><b>Counter-Strike</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Half-Life: Counter-Strike</b></i> or <i><b>Counter-Strike 1.6</b></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> video game developed by <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve</a>. It was initially developed and released as a <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Game_mod" class="mw-redirect" title="Game mod">modification</a> by <a href="/wiki/Minh_Le" title="Minh Le">Minh "Gooseman" Le</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jess_Cliffe" title="Jess Cliffe">Jess Cliffe</a> in 1999, before Le and Cliffe were hired and the game's intellectual property acquired. <i>Counter-Strike</i> was released by Valve for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> in 2000. The game is the first installment in the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike" title="Counter-Strike"><i>Counter-Strike</i> series</a>. Several <a href="/wiki/Video_game_remake" title="Video game remake">remakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_(video_gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Port (video gaming)">ports</a> have been released on <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. </p>
      <p>Set in various locations around the globe, players assume the roles of <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorist</a> forces and <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorist</a> militants opposing them. During each round of gameplay, the two teams are tasked with defeating the other by the means of either achieving the map's objectives or eliminating all of the enemy combatants. Each player may customize their arsenal of weapons and accessories at the beginning of every match, with currency being earned after the end of each round. </p>

      <p><i>Counter-Strike</i> is a first-person shooter game in which players join either the terrorist team, the counter-terrorist team, or become spectators. Each team attempts to complete their mission objective and/or eliminate the opposing team. Each round starts with the two teams <a href="/wiki/Spawning_(computer_gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spawning (computer gaming)">spawning</a> simultaneously. All players have only one life by default and start with a pistol as well as a knife. </p>
      <p>The objectives vary depending on the type of map, and these are the most usual ones: </p> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li><b>Bomb defusal</b>: The terrorists must carry a bomb, plant it on one of the designated spots and protect it from being disarmed by the counter-terrorists before it explodes to win. The counter-terrorists win if the time runs out with no conclusion.</li> 
         <li><b>Hostage rescue</b>: The counter-terrorists must rescue a group of hostages held by the terrorists to win. The terrorists win if the time runs out with no conclusion.</li> 
         <li><b>Assassination</b>: One of the counter-terrorists is chosen to act as a VIP and the team must escort this player to a designated spot on the map to win the game. The terrorists win if the VIP is killed or if the time runs out with no conclusion.</li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl> 
      <p>A player can choose to play as one of eight different default character models (four for each side, although <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Condition_Zero" title="Counter-Strike: Condition Zero">Counter-Strike: Condition Zero</a></i> added two extra models, bringing the total to ten). Players are generally given a few seconds before the round begins (known as "freeze time") to prepare and buy equipment, during which they cannot attack or move. They can return to the buy area within a set amount of time to buy more equipment (some custom maps included neutral "buy zones" that could be used by both teams). Once the round has ended, surviving players retain their equipment for use in the next round; players who were killed begin the next round with the basic default starting equipment. </p>
      <p>Standard monetary bonuses are awarded for winning a round, losing a round, killing an enemy, being the first to instruct a hostage to follow, rescuing a hostage, planting the bomb (Terrorist) or defusing the bomb (Counter-Terrorist). </p>
      <p>The scoreboard displays team scores in addition to statistics for each player: name, kills, deaths, and <a href="/wiki/Ping_(video_gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ping (video gaming)">ping</a> (in milliseconds). The scoreboard also indicates whether a player is dead, carrying the bomb (on bomb maps), or is the VIP (on assassination maps), although information on players on the opposing team is hidden from a player until their death, as this information can be important. </p>
      <p>Killed players become "spectators" for the duration of the round; they cannot change their names before their next spawn, text chat cannot be sent to or received from live players, and voice chat can only be received from live players and not sent to them. Spectators are generally able to watch the rest of the round from multiple selectable views, although some servers disable some of these views to prevent dead players from relaying information about living players to their teammates through alternative media (most notably voice in the case of <a href="/wiki/Internet_cafe" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet cafe">Internet cafes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">Voice over IP</a> programs such as <a href="/wiki/TeamSpeak" title="TeamSpeak">TeamSpeak</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ventrilo" title="Ventrilo">Ventrilo</a>). This form of cheating is known as "ghosting." </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><i>Counter-Strike</i> began as a <a href="/wiki/Mod_(video_gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mod (video gaming)">mod</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s engine <a href="/wiki/GoldSrc" title="GoldSrc">GoldSrc</a>. Minh Le, the mod's co-creator, had started his last semester at university, and wanted to do something in game development to help give him better job prospects. Throughout university, Le had worked on mods with the <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i> engine, and on looking for this latest project, wanted to try something new and opted for GoldSrc. At the onset, Valve had not yet released the <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kit</a> (SDK) for GoldSrc but affirmed it would be available in a few months, allowing Le to work on the character models in the interim. Once the GoldSrc SDK was available, Le estimated it took him about a month and a half to complete the programming and integrate his models for "Beta One" of <i>Counter-Strike</i>. To assist, Le had help from <a href="/wiki/Jess_Cliffe" title="Jess Cliffe">Jess Cliffe</a> who managed the game's website and community, and had contacts within level map making community to help build some of the levels for the game.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_le_2001_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_le_2001-7">[5]</a></sup> The theme of countering terrorists was inspired by Le's own interest in guns and the military, and from games like <i>Rainbow Six</i> and <i>Spec Ops</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_le_2001_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_le_2001-7">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Le and Cliffe continued to release Betas on a frequent basis for feedback. The initial few Betas, released starting in June 1999, had limited audiences but by the fifth one, interest in the project dramatically grew.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra_le_2001_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra_le_2001-7">[5]</a></sup> The interest in the game drew numerous players to the website, which helped Le and Cliffe to make revenue from ads hosted on the site.<sup id="cite_ref-retro_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retro-8">[6]</a></sup> Around 2000 at the time of Beta 5's release, the two were approached by Valve, offering to buy the <i>Counter-Strike</i> <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> and offering both jobs to continue its development.<sup id="cite_ref-retro_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retro-8">[6]</a></sup> Both accepted the offer, and by September 2000, Valve released the first non-beta version of the game. While Cliffe stayed with Valve, Le did some additional work towards a <i>Counter-Strike 2.0</i> based on Valve's upcoming <a href="/wiki/Source_(game_engine)" title="Source (game engine)">Source engine</a>, but left to start his own studio after Valve opted to shelve the sequel.<sup id="cite_ref-retro_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retro-8">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><i>Counter-Strike</i> itself is a mod, and it has developed its own community of script writers and mod creators. Some mods add <a href="/wiki/Computer_game_bot" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game bot">bots</a>, while others remove features of the game, and others create different modes of play. Some mods, often called "admin plugins", give server administrators more flexible and efficient control over their servers. There are some mods which affect gameplay heavily, such as Gun Game, where players start with a basic pistol and must score kills to receive better weapons, and Zombie Mod, where one team consists of zombies and must "spread the infection" by killing the other team (using only the knife). There are also Superhero mods which mix the first-person gameplay of <i>Counter-Strike</i> with an experience system, allowing a player to become more powerful as they continue to play. The game is highly customizable on the player's end, allowing the user to install or even create their own custom skins, HUDs, spray graphics, sprites, and sound effects, given the proper tools.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Valve_Anti-Cheat">Valve Anti-Cheat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Valve Anti-Cheat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Valve_Anti-Cheat" title="Valve Anti-Cheat">Valve Anti-Cheat</a>
      </div> 
      <p><i>Counter-Strike</i> has been a target for <a href="/wiki/Cheating_in_online_games" title="Cheating in online games">cheating in online games</a> since its release. In-game, cheating is often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (computer security)">hacking</a>" in reference to programs or "<a href="/wiki/Hacker_(hobbyist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (hobbyist)">hacks</a>" executed by the client. Valve has implemented an anti-cheat system called <a href="/wiki/Valve_Anti-Cheat" title="Valve Anti-Cheat">Valve Anti-Cheat</a> (VAC). Players cheating on a VAC-enabled server risk having their account permanently banned from all VAC-secured servers. </p>
      <p>With the first version of VAC, a ban took hold almost instantly after being detected and the cheater had to wait two years to have the account unbanned. Since VAC's second version, cheaters are not banned automatically. With the second version, Valve instituted a policy of 'delayed bans,' the theory being that if a new hack is developed which circumvents the VAC system, it will spread amongst the 'cheating' community. By delaying the initial ban, Valve hopes to identify and ban as many cheaters as possible. Like any software detection system, some cheats are not detected by VAC. To remedy this, some servers implement a voting system, in which case players can call for a vote to kick or ban the accused cheater. VAC's success at identifying cheats and banning those who use them has also provided a boost in the purchasing of private cheats.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> These cheats are updated frequently to minimize the risk of detection, and are generally only available to a trusted list of recipients who collectively promise not to reveal the underlying design. Even with private cheats however, some servers have alternative anticheats to coincide with VAC itself. This can help with detecting some cheaters, but most paid for cheats are designed to bypass these alternative server-based anticheats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>When <i>Counter-Strike</i> was published by <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Entertainment" title="Sierra Entertainment">Sierra Studios</a>, it was bundled with <i><a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_Classic" title="Team Fortress Classic">Team Fortress Classic</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life:_Opposing_Force" title="Half-Life: Opposing Force">Opposing Force</a></i> multiplayer, and the <i>Wanted</i>, <i>Half-Life: Absolute Redemption</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Firearms_(video_game)" title="Firearms (video game)">Firearms</a></i> mods.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_Review_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_Review-10">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On March 24, 1999, <a href="/wiki/Planet_Half-Life" title="Planet Half-Life">Planet Half-Life</a> opened its <i>Counter-Strike</i> section. Within two weeks, the site had received 10,000 hits. On June 19, 1999, the first public beta of <i>Counter-Strike</i> was released, followed by numerous further "beta" releases. On April 12, 2000, <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve</a> announced that the <i>Counter-Strike</i> developers and Valve had teamed up. In January 2013, Valve began testing a version of <i>Counter-Strike</i> for <a href="/wiki/OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, eventually releasing the update to all users in April 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Upon its retail release, <i>Counter-Strike</i> received highly favorable reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_Review_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN_Review-10">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GameRankings_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameRankings-13">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-15">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GameRev_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameRev-17">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GameSpot_Review_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GameSpot_Review-18">[16]</a></sup> In 2003, <i>Counter-Strike</i> was inducted into <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a>'s list of the greatest games of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-Gamespot_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gamespot-23">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported that E-Sports Entertainment <a href="/wiki/ESEA_League" title="ESEA League">ESEA League</a> started the first professional fantasy e-sports league in 2004 with the game <i>Counter-Strike</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> Some credit the move into professional competitive team play with prizes as a major factor in <i>Counter-Strike</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> longevity and success.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Global retail sales of <i>Counter-Strike</i> surpassed 250,000 units by July 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-cgwsales2_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cgwsales2-27">[25]</a></sup> and 1.5 million by December 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-cgwsales3_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cgwsales3-28">[26]</a></sup> In the United States, its retail version sold 550,000 copies and earned $15.7 million by August 2006, after its release in November 2000. It was the country's 22nd best-selling PC game between January 2000 and August 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-edgesales_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edgesales-29">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Xbox version sold 1.5 million copies in total.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian_sale_ban">Brazilian sale ban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brazilian sale ban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On January 17, 2008, a Brazilian federal court order prohibiting all sales of <i>Counter-Strike</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/EverQuest" title="EverQuest">EverQuest</a></i> began to be enforced. The federal Brazilian judge Carlos Alberto Simões de Tomaz ordered the ban in October 2007 because, as argued by the judge, the games "bring imminent stimulus to the subversion of the social order, attempting against the democratic state and the law and against public security."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> As of June 18, 2009, a <a href="/wiki/Regional_Federal_Courts" title="Regional Federal Courts">regional federal court</a> order lifting the prohibition on the sale of <i>Counter-Strike</i> was published. The game is now being sold again in Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitive_play">Competitive play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Competitive play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike_in_esports" title="Counter-Strike in esports">Counter-Strike in esports</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The original <i>Counter-Strike</i> has been played in tournaments since 2000 with the first major being hosted in 2001 at the <a href="/wiki/Cyberathlete_Professional_League" title="Cyberathlete Professional League">Cyberathlete Professional League</a> Winter Championship.<sup id="cite_ref-espo_Espo_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espo_Espo-35">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-blog_AneS_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog_AneS-36">[34]</a></sup> The first official sequel was <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Source" title="Counter-Strike: Source">Counter-Strike: Source</a></i>, released on November 1, 2004. The game was criticized by the competitive community, who believed the game's skill ceiling was significantly lower than that of CS 1.6. This caused a divide in the competitive community as to which game to play competitively.<sup id="cite_ref-Kane2008_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kane2008-37">[35]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequels">Sequels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Strike_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sequels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike" title="Counter-Strike">Counter-Strike</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Following the success of the first <i>Counter-Strike</i>, Valve went on to make multiple sequels to the game. <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Condition_Zero" title="Counter-Strike: Condition Zero">Counter-Strike: Condition Zero</a></i>, a game using <i>Counter-Strike</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/GoldSrc" title="GoldSrc">GoldSrc</a> engine, was released in 2004. <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Source" title="Counter-Strike: Source">Counter-Strike: Source</a></i>, a remake of the original <i>Counter-Strike</i>, was the first in the series to use Valve's <a href="/wiki/Source_(game_engine)" title="Source (game engine)">Source engine</a> and was also released in 2004, eight months after the release of <i>Counter-Strike: Condition Zero</i>. The next game in the <i>Counter-Strike</i> series to be developed primarily by Valve was <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike:_Global_Offensive" title="Counter-Strike: Global Offensive">Counter-Strike: Global Offensive</a></i>, released for Windows, OS X, Linux, <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a> in 2012. </p>
      <p>The game spawned multiple spin-offs for the Asian gaming market. The first, <i>Counter-Strike Neo</i>, was an arcade game developed by <a href="/wiki/Namco" title="Namco">Namco</a> and released in Japan in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup> In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Nexon_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nexon Corporation">Nexon Corporation</a> released <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike_Online" title="Counter-Strike Online">Counter-Strike Online</a></i>, a free-to-play instalment in the series monetized via <a href="/wiki/Microtransaction" title="Microtransaction">microtransactions</a>. <i>Counter-Strike Online</i> was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike_Online_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-Strike Online 2">Counter-Strike Online 2</a></i> in 2013. In 2014, Nexon released <i><a href="/wiki/Counter-Strike_Nexon:_Zombies" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-Strike Nexon: Zombies">Counter-Strike Nexon: Zombies</a></i> worldwide via <a href="/wiki/Steam_(service)" title="Steam (service)">Steam</a>. </p>
