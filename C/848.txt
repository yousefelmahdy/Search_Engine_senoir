      <p>An <b>intelligent designer</b>, also referred to as an <b>intelligent agent</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothetical</a> <a href="/wiki/Will_(philosophy)" title="Will (philosophy)">willed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-awareness" title="Self-awareness">self-aware</a> entity that the <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design_movement" title="Intelligent design movement">intelligent design movement</a> argues had some role in the <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">origin</a> and/or development of life. The term "intelligent cause" is also used, implying their <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleological</a> supposition of direction and purpose in features of the universe and of living things. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_designer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The popularly termed <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design_movement" title="Intelligent design movement">intelligent design movement</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Neo-creationist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-creationist">neo-creationist</a> campaign that arose out of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_fundamentalism" title="Christian fundamentalism">Christian fundamentalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Creation_science" title="Creation science">creation science</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Proponents of <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design" title="Intelligent design">intelligent design</a> argue to the public that their concept does not posit the identity of the designer as part of this effort, but in statements to their constituency, which consists largely of <a href="/wiki/Christian_right" title="Christian right">Christian conservatives</a>, they identify the designer as <a href="/wiki/God#Biblical_definition_of_God" title="God">God</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-debate_won_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-debate_won-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dembski_logos_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dembski_logos-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ruling_context_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruling_context-7">[7]</a></sup> The Discovery Institute has claimed that university criticism of intelligent design is tantamount to "endorsement of an anti-religious view"<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">Identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_designer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/William_Dembski" class="mw-redirect" title="William Dembski">William Dembski</a> states in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Design_Inference" class="mw-redirect" title="Design Inference">Design Inference</a></i> that the nature of the intelligent designer cannot be inferred from intelligent design<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and suggests that the designer, if one is even necessary for design inference, may or may not be "the God of Scripture."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In December 2007 Dembski told <a href="/wiki/Focus_on_the_Family" title="Focus on the Family">Focus on the Family</a>, "I believe God created the world for a purpose. The Designer of intelligent design is, ultimately, the Christian God."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Some leading intelligent design proponents have stated identifying or characterizing the designer is beyond the scope of intelligent design as a line of inquiry. Proponents had hoped that, by avoiding invoking <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation</a> by a specific supernatural entity, (such as that employed by <a href="/wiki/Creation_science" title="Creation science">creation science</a>), intelligent design would be considered scientific and not violate the <a href="/wiki/Establishment_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Establishment clause">establishment clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/US_constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="US constitution">US constitution</a>. Proponents feared that were intelligent design identified as a restatement of previous forms of creationism, it would be precluded from being taught in public schools after the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Edwards_vs_Aguillard" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwards vs Aguillard">Edwards vs Aguillard</a></i>. This line of reasoning was not particularly persuasive to most in the scientific community, which overwhelmingly rejected intelligent design as both a line of scientific inquiry and as a basis for a sound education in science. </p>
      <p>On December 20, 2005 <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_courts" class="mw-redirect" title="United States federal courts">federal district court</a> ruled in <i><a href="/wiki/Kitzmiller_v._Dover_Area_School_District" title="Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District">Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District</a></i> that intelligent design was not science and was essentially religious in nature. The ruling not only rendered that public school district's endorsement of intelligent design as an alternative to evolution in science classes unconstitutional on the grounds that its inclusion violates the <a href="/wiki/Establishment_Clause" title="Establishment Clause">Establishment Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a>, but validated the objections of critics who discounted proponents' claim that the identity was not God. </p>
      <p>Highlighting these mutually exclusive claims about the designer, Dembski, despite having said that the intelligent designer or designers could be any <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">god</a> or gods, or even <a href="/wiki/Space_alien" class="mw-redirect" title="Space alien">space aliens</a>, has also said that "intelligent design should be understood as the evidence that God has placed in nature to show that the physical world is the product of intelligence and not simply the result of mindless material forces"<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and that "Intelligent design is just the Logos theology of John's Gospel restated in the idiom of information theory."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Behe" title="Michael Behe">Michael Behe</a>, in his book <a href="/wiki/Darwin%27s_Black_Box" title="Darwin's Black Box">Darwin's Black Box</a>, suggested the designer might be a <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">time traveling</a> cell biologist.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>At various times, leading proponents in the intelligent design movement have clearly expressed that they consider the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Abrahamic God">Abrahamic God</a> "<a href="/wiki/Elohim" title="Elohim">Elohim</a>" in his role as a <a href="/wiki/Creator_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Creator God">creator God</a>, to be the intelligent designer and denied that intelligent designer is God, depending on which audience they are addressing. One example is William Dembski, who on his blog in response to the question "Is the designer responsible for biological complexity God?" said "not necessarily" and "To ask who or what is the designer of a particular object is to ask for the immediate intelligent agent responsible for its design. The point is that God is able to work through derived or surrogate intelligences, which can be anything from angels to organizing principles embedded in nature."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Yet to the intelligent design movement's conservative Christian constituents Dembski has said "intelligent design should be understood as the evidence that God has placed in nature to show that the physical world is the product of intelligence and not simply the result of mindless material forces. This evidence is available to all apart from the special revelation of God in salvation history as recounted in Scripture. ... Intelligent design makes it impossible to be an intellectually fulfilled atheist. This gives intelligent design incredible traction as a tool for apologetics, opening up the God-question to individuals who think that science has buried God"<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and "Thus, in its relation to Christianity, intelligent design should be viewed as a ground-clearing operation that gets rid of the intellectual rubbish that for generations has kept Christianity from receiving serious consideration."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_C._Meyer" title="Stephen C. Meyer">Stephen C. Meyer</a>, founder and leader of the intelligent design program of the Discovery Institute admitted on national television he believes that the designer is God.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Phillip_E._Johnson" title="Phillip E. Johnson">Phillip E. Johnson</a>, considered the father of the ID movement has stated the goal of the intelligent design movement: </p>

Johnson">Phillip E.       <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Claimed_actions">Claimed actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_designer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Claimed actions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Opinion as to the amount of creation the intelligent designer has done varies within the ID movement. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Behe" title="Michael Behe">Michael Behe</a>'s concept of <a href="/wiki/Irreducible_complexity" title="Irreducible complexity">irreducible complexity</a> has <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> accounting for most of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> but the intelligent designer contributing the design of some <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. Others in the ID movement however contest concepts such as <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common descent</a>, particularly of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">apes</a>. Though most in the ID movement seem to be <a href="/wiki/Old_Earth_Creationist" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Earth Creationist">Old Earth Creationists</a>, a few are <a href="/wiki/Young_Earth_Creationist" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Earth Creationist">Young Earth Creationists</a> who believe in <a href="/wiki/Ex-nihilo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ex-nihilo">ex-nihilo</a>. </p>
      <p>The amount of creation that the intelligent designer did has also been criticised by <a href="/wiki/Young_Earth_Creationist" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Earth Creationist">Young Earth Creationists</a> as not being specific enough, and particularly contradicting their beliefs of <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inerrancy" title="Biblical inerrancy">Biblical inerrancy</a> and a young earth.<sup id="cite_ref-aig_views_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aig_views-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-morris_design_not_enough_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris_design_not_enough-23">[23]</a></sup> Some intelligent design proponents say the intelligent designer <a href="/wiki/Fine-tuned_universe" title="Fine-tuned universe">fine-tuned the universe's</a> <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">physical constants</a> in such a way that life is the result of the universe's physical constants being related to one another in a fashion that permits life to exist. The <a href="/wiki/Fine-tuned_universe" title="Fine-tuned universe">fine-tuned universe argument</a> is a central premise or presented as a given in many of the published works of prominent intelligent design proponents, such as <a href="/wiki/William_A._Dembski" title="William A. Dembski">William A. Dembski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Behe" title="Michael Behe">Michael Behe</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_designer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Intelligent design has been presented by its proponents as a "big tent" strategy into which several accounts of creation can fit. Were a "scientific" version of intelligent design approved for inclusion in public school science curricula, then a path would be opened for discussion of alternatives to not only <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> but <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)" title="Naturalism (philosophy)">naturalism</a> as well, and eventually religious accounts on the <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a>. The vast majority of scientists reject the concept of intelligent design and an intelligent designer. Instead, the most widely accepted explanation is that physical processes such as natural selection can account for the complexity of life and other phenomena and features of the universe. Attempts to insert theories of intelligent design into public school science curricula fits in with the intelligent design movement's social aims, via the overturning of Western secularism as detailed in the <a href="/wiki/Wedge_strategy" title="Wedge strategy">Wedge strategy</a>. The concept of the intelligent designer has been criticised as a <a href="/wiki/God_of_the_gaps#The_&quot;God-of-the-gaps_argument&quot;_in_modern_usage" title="God of the gaps">God-of-the-gaps</a> argument. Introducing the hypothesis of an intelligent designer introduces the unsolved problem of accounting for the origin of such a designer (<a href="/wiki/First_cause" class="mw-redirect" title="First cause">first cause</a>). </p>
      <p>By raising the question of the need for a designer for objects with irreducible complexity, intelligent design also raises the question, "what designed the designer?" <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> has argued that "If complex organisms demand an explanation, so does a complex designer. And it's no solution to raise the theologian's plea that God (or the intelligent designer) is simply immune to the normal demands of scientific explanation,"<sup id="cite_ref-dawkins_complexdesigner_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dawkins_complexdesigner-24">[24]</a></sup> since such an answer would be unscientific. With religious creationism, the question "what created God?" can be answered with theological arguments, but in intelligent design, the chain of designers can be followed back indefinitely in an <a href="/wiki/Turtles_all_the_way_down" title="Turtles all the way down">infinite regression</a>, leaving the question of the creation of the first designer dangling. As a result, intelligent design does not explain how the complexity happened in the first place; it just moves it.<sup id="cite_ref-dawkins_time_2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dawkins_time_2-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Elliott_Sober" title="Elliott Sober">Elliott Sober</a> says that by intelligent design's own arguments, a designer capable of creating irreducible complexity must also be irreducibly complex: "Any mind in nature that designs and builds an irreducibly complex system is itself irreducibly complex"<sup id="cite_ref-sober_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sober-26">[26]</a></sup> Sober says that this an argument that intelligent design proponents still need to respond to. </p>
      <p>If intelligent design proponents invoke an <a href="/wiki/Uncaused_causer" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncaused causer">uncaused causer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> to resolve this problem,<sup id="cite_ref-wdd2_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wdd2-27">[27]</a></sup> they contradict a fundamental assumption of intelligent design that design requires a designer<sup id="cite_ref-fundamental_assumption_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundamental_assumption-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sc_indicates_design_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sc_indicates_design-29">[29]</a></sup> and reduce intelligent design to religious creationism. Another possible counter-argument might be an <a href="/wiki/Turtles_all_the_way_down" title="Turtles all the way down">infinite regression</a> of designers. However, admitting infinite numbers of objects also allows any arbitrarily improbable event to occur,<sup id="cite_ref-arbitrarily_improbable_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arbitrarily_improbable-30">[30]</a></sup> such as an object with "specific" complexity assembling itself by chance. Again, this contradicts a fundamental assumption of intelligent design that a designer is needed for every specifically complex object, producing a logical contradiction. </p>
      <p>Critics contend the claim that positing a designer which explains gaps in our understanding yet does not need to be itself explained as not a contribution to knowledge but as a <a href="/wiki/Thought-terminating_clich%C3%A9" title="Thought-terminating cliché">thought-terminating cliché</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenhouse_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenhouse-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dover_trial">The Dover trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_designer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Dover trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 2005, intelligent design proponents arguments regarding the identity of a designer became an issue considered by the court in <i><a href="/wiki/Kitzmiller_v._Dover_Area_School_District" title="Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District">Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District</a></i>, the "Dover trial," where plaintiffs successfully argued that intelligent design is a form of creationism, and that the school board policy requiring the presentation of intelligent design as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> as an "explanation of the origin of life" thus violated the <a href="/wiki/Establishment_Clause" title="Establishment Clause">Establishment Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment to the United States Constitution</a>. In his ruling, the judge, John E. Jones III, stated </p> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>"However, as Dr. Haught testified, anyone familiar with Western religious thought would immediately make the association that the tactically unnamed designer is God..." -- <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Kitzmiller_v._Dover_Area_School_District/4:Whether_ID_Is_Science#Page_25_of_139" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District/4:Whether ID Is Science"><i>Ruling, Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District, page 25</i></a></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Jones also commented that the appearance of design is <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjective</a>: </p> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>"It is readily apparent to the Court that the only attribute of design that biological systems appear to share with human artifacts is their complex appearance, i.e. if it looks complex or designed, it must have been designed. (23:73 (Behe)). This inference to design based upon the appearance of a "purposeful arrangement of parts" is a completely subjective proposition, determined in the eye of each beholder and his/her viewpoint concerning the complexity of a system." -- <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Kitzmiller_v._Dover_Area_School_District/4:Whether_ID_Is_Science#Page_81_of_139" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District/4:Whether ID Is Science"><i>Ruling, Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District, page 81</i></a></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>"For human artifacts, we know the designer's identity, human, and the mechanism of design, as we have experience based upon empirical evidence that humans can make such things, as well as many other attributes including the designer's abilities, needs, and desires. With ID, proponents assert that they refuse to propose hypotheses on the designer's identity, do not propose a mechanism, and the designer, he/she/it/they, has never been seen. In that vein, defense expert Professor Minnich agreed that in the case of human artifacts and objects, we know the identity and capacities of the human designer, but we do not know any of those attributes for the designer of biological life. In addition, Professor Behe agreed that for the design of human artifacts, we know the designer and its attributes and we have a baseline for human design that does not exist for design of biological systems. Professor Behe's only response to these seemingly insurmountable points of disanalogy was that the inference still works in science fiction movies. -- <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Kitzmiller_v._Dover_Area_School_District/4:Whether_ID_Is_Science#Page_81_of_139" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District/4:Whether ID Is Science"><i>Ruling, Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District, page 81</i></a></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>The judge ruled that "ID cannot uncouple itself from its creationist, and thus religious, antecedents"<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and "that ID is an interesting theological argument, but that it is not science."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>
