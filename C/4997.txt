       <h1><a id="top" name="top"></a> <a id="content2" name="content"></a>Firearms Excise Tax FAQs</h1> 
       <p><strong>NOTICE</strong>: TTB has temporarily removed its "Frequently Asked Questions" for Firearms and Ammunition Excise Tax (FAET). These FAQs are under review to ensure that they are current and address issues relevant to FAET taxpayers. The FAET FAQs will be re-posted once the review and any necessary updates are complete.</p> 
       <p>In the meantime, guidance on FAET can be found in TTB's online "FAET Reference Guide," which provides information on similar topics in an outline format. The reference guide is available here: <a href="https://www.ttb.gov/firearms/reference-guide">https://www.ttb.gov/firearms/reference-guide</a>.</p> 
       <p>Any additional questions can be submitted to TTB's National Revenue Center through the contact form available here: <a href="https://www.ttb.gov/contact-nrc">https://www.ttb.gov/contact-nrc</a>.</p>

		<h2 align="center">
			<a id="Small" name="Small"></a>Small Manufacturers Exemptions</h2>
		<div id="menu1" class="mmenu"><strong>What was the effect of the amendment to U.S.C. 4182 regarding the 50 gun exemption from Federal Firearms and Ammunition Excise Taxes?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub1" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>As of October 1, 2005, any pistol, revolver, or firearm that was manufactured, produced or imported by a person who manufactures, produces or imports less than an aggregate of 50 such articles during the calendar year is now exempt from the tax.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu2" class="mmenu"><strong>How does a manufacturer, producer or importer determine eligibility under the new 50 gun exemption?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub2" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>A manufacturer, importer or producer qualifies for this exemption based on the number of firearms manufactured, produced and imported on a calendar year basis (January 1-December 31). For example, if Company "A" manufactures 20 firearms in Year 1 then it is exempt from FAET on all firearms it manufactures and sells in Year 1. However, if Company "A" manufactures 55 firearms in Year 2 then it is subject to FAET on all firearms sold in Year 2.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu3" class="mmenu"><strong>How are controlled groups affected by the 50 gun exemption?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub3" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Since all persons that are a part of a controlled group of corporations will be treated as one taxpayer for purposes of this exemption, members of the group are only exempt from the tax if, collectively, they manufacture, produce or import less than 50 articles during the calendar year.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu4" class="mmenu"><strong>Does the 50 gun exemption also apply to shells and cartridges?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub4" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>No, it does not.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu5" class="mmenu"><strong>May I apply this exemption to transactions that occurred prior to October 1, 2005?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub5" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>No, you may not.</p>
			</div>
		</div>

		<h2 align="center">Background
			<a id="background" name="background"></a>
		</h2>
		<div id="menu6" class="mmenu"><strong>What is Firearms and Ammunition Excise Tax (FAET)?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub6" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Firearms and Ammunition Excise Tax (FAET) is a tax imposed by Chapter 32 of the Internal Revenue Code (<a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4181.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4181</a>) on the sale of firearms and ammunition by manufacturers, producers, and importers.</p>
				<p>Chapter 32 also imposes taxes on other commodities such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Tires</li>
					<li>Gasoline</li>
					<li>Coal</li>
					<li>Vaccines</li>
					<li>Sport fishing equipment</li>
					<li>Bows and arrows</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu7" class="mmenu"><strong>What agency collects FAET? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub7" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Since its inception in 1919, different Federal agencies have been responsible for overseeing the collection of FAET. From 1919 to 1990, the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) was responsible for collecting FAET. On January 1, 1991 , this function was transferred from the IRS to the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms (ATF).</p>
				<p>On November 25, 2002 , President Bush signed into law the Homeland Security Act of 2002. The Homeland Security Act divided the functions of ATF into two new agencies: the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms, and Explosives (ATF) in the Department of Justice and the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB) in the Department of the Treasury. On January 24, 2003 , TTB became responsible for administering the collection of FAET and handling the regulatory and taxation aspects of the alcohol and tobacco industries. The TTB's National Revenue Center performs all duties supporting the collection of FAET.</p>
				<p>" [Refer to <a href="/images/industry_circulars/archives/2003/03-02.html" target="_blank">Industry Circular 2003-2</a>] " and the link should link to that particular page.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu8" class="mmenu"><strong>What Federal laws and regulations pertain to FAET? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub8" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>The following Federal laws pertain to FAET:</p>
				<ul>
					<li><a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/stDch32.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. Chapter 32 (Manufacturers Excise Taxes)</a></li>
					<li><a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-F/chapter-61" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. Chapter 61 (Information and Returns)</a></li>
					<li><a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-F/chapter-62" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. Chapter 62 (Time and Place for Paying Tax</a></li>
					<li><a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-F/chapter-63" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. Chapter 63 (Assessment)</a></li>

		<h2 align="center">
			<a id="claims" name="claims"></a>Claims</h2>
		<div id="menu13" class="mmenu"><strong>When may I file a claim?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub13" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You may file a claim when you overpay your FAET.</p>
				<p>[Refer to 27 <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=d9c1f8a26e2d2afc3f6cbc2133eb20ed&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.9.615.13&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CFR 53.171</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu14" class="mmenu"><strong>What is a credit?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub14" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>A credit is an adjustment to your tax payment that decreases the amount of FAET you owe. This occurs when you overpay your FAET.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu15" class="mmenu"><strong>What is a refund?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub15" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>A refund is money returned to you when you overpay your FAET.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu16" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I file a claim for credit?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub16" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You may file for a credit by explaining your overpayment(s) in Schedule C on your tax return (<a href="/images/pdfs/forms/f530026.pdf" target="_blank">TTB F 5300.26</a>).</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu17" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I file a claim for refund?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub17" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You may file for a refund by completing <a href="/images/pdfs/forms/f56208.pdf" target="_blank">TTB F 2635 (5620.8)</a>.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu18" class="mmenu"><strong>Does a statute of limitations apply if I file a claim?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub18" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Yes. You must file for your credit or refund within 3 years from the date your filed your return. However, if you did not file a tax return, you must file for your credit or refund within 2 years from the date you paid your FAET.</p>
			</div>
		</div>

		<h2 align="center">
			<a id="employer_id" name="employer_id"></a>Employer Identification Number</h2>
		<div id="menu19" class="mmenu"><strong>What is an Employer Identification Number (EIN)?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub19" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>An EIN is a nine-digit number that the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) assigns to businesses and entities for tax filing. Some examples include:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Sole proprietors</li>
					<li>Corporations</li>
					<li>Partnerships</li>
					<li>Estates</li>
					<li>Trusts</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu20" class="mmenu"><strong>When do I need to obtain an EIN?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub20" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You must obtain an EIN if you manufacture, produce, or import articles on a regular basis. However, you do not need to obtain an EIN if you manufacture, produce, or import articles on a one-time or occasional basis. If you manufacture, produce, or import articles on a one-time or occasional basis, TTB will establish your account through your Social Security Number instead.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu21" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I obtain an EIN?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub21" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You can obtain an EIN by filing a <a href="http://www.irs.gov/businesses/small/article/0,,id=102767,00.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Form SS-4</a> with the IRS. You can obtain an SS-4 by contacting your local IRS office.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu22" class="mmenu"><strong>When do I use my Employer Identification Number (EIN) or Social Security Number (SSN)?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub22" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Include your EIN on all returns and correspondence that you file with TTB. In addition, include your EIN on all check and money order payments you make to TTB. If you do not have an EIN because you manufacture, produce, or import articles on a one-time or occasional basis, use your Social Security Number instead.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu23" class="mmenu"><strong>What if my EIN changes?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub23" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If your EIN changes, contact the <a href="/who-we-are/contact-us">FAET Unit</a> at the NRC and provide them with your new EIN.</p>
			</div>
		</div>

		<div id="sub25" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You must submit an Annual Firearms Manufacturing and Exportation Report (<a href="https://www.atf.gov/firearms/docs/form/annual-firearms-manufacturing-and-exportation-report-afmer-atf-form-530011/download" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATF F 5300.11</a>) to notify ATF of all weapons (other than semi-automatic weapons) that you manufacture. You must submit an ATF Form 5300.11A to report all semi-automatic weapons that you manufacture.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu26" class="mmenu"><strong>When is my Annual Manufacturing and Exportation Report due? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub26" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Your Annual Manufacturing and Exportation Report is due by April 1 of each year. Your report will cover the previous year's manufacturing activities. You must file a report every year (even if you do not manufacture firearms during the year).</p>
			</div>

Some examples of financial records include:</p>

		<div id="menu35" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET if I import an article for someone else?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub35" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>The person who actually causes the importation of the article is liable for FAET.</p>
				<p><em>Scenario: </em>I am a Federal Firearms Licensee. Since I have a Federal Firearms License, a customer asks me to special order a firearm from France and import it into the United States. I complete the required paperwork, import the firearm, and sell the firearm to my customer. The customer reimburses me for customs duties and fees. Who is liable for FAET in this instance?</p>
				<em>Answer: </em>In this instance, the customer is liable for FAET since he actually caused the importation of the firearm (He would be referred to as the <em>beneficial owner</em>). Since you merely acted as an agent, and imported the firearm on behalf of the customer, you are not liable for FAET (You would be referred to as the <em>nominal importer</em>). [Refer to <a href="/images/pdfs/rulings/69-393.html" target="_blank">Revenue Ruling 69-393</a> and <a href="/images/pdfs/rulings/72-215.html" target="_blank">Revenue Ruling 72-215</a>]</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu36" class="mmenu"><strong>If I import an article for my personal use, how do I determine my FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub36" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If you import an article for personal use, you calculate your FAET by using one of the following two options:</p>
				<p><strong>(1) Calculate the sale price on your own.</strong></p>
				<p>Your sale price will be the total of the following charges:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>The entry value that Customs uses to determine their duty;</li>
					<li>The import duty;</li>
					<li>Customs handling fees;</li>
					<li>Freight and delivery charges to the port of entry; and</li>
					<li>Any other costs of importing.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>Note: If the entry is duty free (i.e., you are a member of the U.S. Armed Forces or the article is valued below the minimum established by Customs), you must still include the duty that would apply if you were importing the article under normal conditions in your calculation. <br /> When calculating your sale price, you do not need to include:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>State or local taxes; or</li>
					<li>Other expenses you incur after importation.</li>
				</ul>
				<p><strong>(2) Ask TTB to calculate your sale price. </strong></p>
				If you ask TTB to determine your sale price, you will need to send us details (such as pictures, purchase invoices, and other written descriptions and evaluations). Please allow several weeks for us to make a determination of your FAET liability.</div>
		</div>

		<h2 align="center">
			<a id="manufacturers" name="manufacturers"></a>Manufacturers and Producers</h2>
		<div id="menu37" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I a manufacturer? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub37" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You are a manufacturer if you manufacture or produce a taxable article from scrap, salvage, junk material, new material, or raw material by:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>processing, manipulating, or changing the form of an article; or</li>
					<li>combining or assembling two or more articles.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to<a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=aebb3765ef60ad90d5ce0325af47a512&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.2.613.1&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> 27 CFR 53.11</a> (definition of manufacturer)]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu38" class="mmenu"><strong>If I manufacture an article and sell it in knockdown condition, am I liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub38" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Yes. If you manufacture an article and sell it in knockdown condition (unassembled but complete as to all component parts), you are liable for FAET.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=aebb3765ef60ad90d5ce0325af47a512&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.2.613.1&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.11</a> (definition of manufacturer)]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu39" class="mmenu"><strong>If I purchase an article in knockdown condition and assemble it, am I liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub39" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If you purchase an article in knockdown condition and assemble it, your liability for FAET will depend on the following factors:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>the amount of labor, material, and overhead required to assemble the article; and</li>
					<li>whether you assemble the article for business or personal use.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=aebb3765ef60ad90d5ce0325af47a512&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.2.613.1&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.11</a> (definition of manufacturer)]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu40" class="mmenu"><strong>If I manufacture an article only for my personal use, am I still liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub40" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If you manufacture a firearm for your personal use, you are not liable for FAET. (Note: this exemption does not apply to partnerships and corporations)</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu41" class="mmenu"><strong>If I manufacture an article and use it in the course of my business, am I liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub41" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Yes. If you manufacture an article and use it in the course of your business, you are liable for tax. FAET will attach on an article once you begin using it during the course of your business. Some examples of business uses of articles include:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Sales samples;</li>
					<li>Demonstration models;</li>
					<li>Use by security force; and</li>
					<li>Use by writers in preparing articles.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4218.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4218</a> , <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=aebb3765ef60ad90d5ce0325af47a512&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.15&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.111</a> , and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=aebb3765ef60ad90d5ce0325af47a512&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.16&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.112</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu42" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I determine my FAET if I manufacture and use an article in the course of my business?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub42" class="subMenu">

		<h2 align="center"><strong>Constructive sale price</strong></h2>
		<div id="menu53" class="mmenu"><strong>What is a constructive sale price (CSP)?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub53" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>A constructive sale price (CSP) is a substitute sale price that the Internal Revenue Code either permits or requires you to use. When you use a CSP, you are constructing a new taxable sale price for your article.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu54" class="mmenu"><strong>When will I use a CSP?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub54" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You will use a CSP when you sell an article:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>At retail;</li>
					<li>On consignment; or</li>
					<li>Otherwise than at arm's length and at less than fair market value (i.e. - sales to affiliated corporations).</li>
				</ul>
				[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4216.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4216(b)</a> and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=6f819bbc6db7244f43e6de9fff735c3a;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.94;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.7.169.4" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.94</a>]</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu55" class="mmenu"><strong>What does "otherwise than at arm's length" mean?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub55" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You qualify as selling an article "otherwise than at arm's length" when:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>You have a special arrangement with the purchaser; <em></em></li>
					<li>You or your purchaser is controlled by the other; or <em></em></li>
					<li>Common control exists between you and your purchaser.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=b55b97efb121229d6adbe6a6b0381b1f&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.5&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.95(d) </a>]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu56" class="mmenu"><strong>What is the purpose of a CSP?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub56" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Since your FAET liability is based on the wholesale price of your article, the Internal Revenue Code established the concept of a CSP to ensure tax fairness.</p>
				<p>For example, when you sell an article at retail or on consignment, your retail sale price will usually be higher than your wholesale price. The Internal Revenue Code permits you to use a CSP so you can establish a sale price for your article that is comparable to your wholesale price.</p>
				<p>However, when you sell an article not at arm's length and less than fair market value, your sale price will usually be lower than if you had sold the article at arm's length and fair market value. In this instance, the Internal Revenue Code requires you to base your FAET on an increased sale price.</p>
				<p><strong>Note</strong>: You should contact the National Revenue Center for information on how to determine your CSP.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<p><a href="#top">Return to Top</a></p>
		<h2 align="center">Price readjustments and exchanges</h2>
		<div id="menu57" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I calculate FAET if I readjust my sale price at a later date?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub57" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You must calculate your FAET on the original sale price of your article. You may only calculate FAET on a readjusted sale price if you make the readjustments before your tax period ends. Some examples of readjustments you may make in your sale price include:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Discounts;</li>
					<li>Rebates;</li>
					<li>Bonuses; and</li>
					<li>Allowances.</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu59" class="mmenu"><strong>May I anticipate price readjustments?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub59" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>No. You may not anticipate readjustments in your sale price when calculating FAET.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=b55b97efb121229d6adbe6a6b0381b1f&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.3&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.93(c) </a>]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu60" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I calculate my FAET if I exchange an article?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub60" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If your customer exchanges or trades in an old article for a new one, you will base your FAET on:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>
						<ul>
							<li>Any allowance for the old article; and</li>
							<li>Any additional amount you charge the customer.</li>
						</ul>
					</li>
				</ul>

		<h2 align="center"><strong>Credit sales and leases</strong></h2>
		<div id="menu61" class="mmenu"><strong>When does FAET attach on a credit sale?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub61" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>When you sell an article on credit, FAET attaches when you actually make the sale ( not when you actually collect the purchase price).</p>
				<p><strong>Example: </strong>I sold a firearm on credit on February 2. I received payment for the firearm on February 10. The customer did not actually take possession of the firearm until February 15.</p>
				<p><strong>Question: </strong>When do I become liable for FAET?</p>
				<p><strong>Answer: </strong>Since you actually transferred title of the firearm to the customer on February 2, you become liable for FAET on February 2.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=b55b97efb121229d6adbe6a6b0381b1f&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.1.613.2&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.2(a) </a>]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu62" class="mmenu"><strong>If I sell an article on credit, but never receive payment, am I still liable for FAET? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub62" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Yes. If you sell an article on credit, but do not actually receive payment from the customer, you are still liable for FAET.</p>
				<p><strong>Example: </strong>I sold a firearm on credit for $200.00. The customer received the firearm, however, I never received payment for the firearm.</p>
				<p><strong>Question: </strong>Do I still owe FAET even though I never received payment?</p>
				<p><strong>Answer: </strong>Yes- you would still owe FAET for the full sale price of $200.00.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=b55b97efb121229d6adbe6a6b0381b1f&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.1.613.2&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.2(c) </a>and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=b55b97efb121229d6adbe6a6b0381b1f&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.8&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.98(c)</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu63" class="mmenu"><strong>What is a lease?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub63" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>A lease is an exclusive and continuous right to possess or use an article for a period of time. A lease may be a verbal or written contract or agreement. This also includes any:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Renewal of a lease;</li>
					<li>Extension of a lease; or</li>
					<li>Subsequent lease of the same article.</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4216.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4216(c)</a> , <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4217.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4217(a)</a> , and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=0471f50d870b6d3696c3868d71ae2806&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.13&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.103</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu64" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET if I lease an article?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub64" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>Yes. A lease is considered a sale.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4217.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4217(a)</a> and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=f57d8525ec1c12da0272848cac415f58&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.13&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.103</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu65" class="mmenu"><strong>When does FAET attach on a lease?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub65" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>FAET attaches on each lease payment made by the customer (lessee).</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4216.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4216(c)</a> and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=f57d8525ec1c12da0272848cac415f58&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.1.613.2&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.2(e)</a> ]</p>
				<p>FAET liability exists for the entire time you lease the article to the customer. You are liable for FAET each time you lease the article. You are also liable for FAET if you sell a previously leased article (regardless as to the amount of tax already paid on previous leases).</p>
				<p>Exception: If you lease an article and you are engaged in the business of selling articles that are the same type and model, you are only liable for FAET until the total tax is paid. Once you have paid the total FAET on a leased article, you are not liable for FAET on any subsequent lease or sale of the same article.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4217.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4217</a> and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=f57d8525ec1c12da0272848cac415f58&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.14&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.104</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu66" class="mmenu"><strong>How do I calculate FAET on a lease?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub66" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>When you lease an article, you must apply a percentage of FAET to each lease payment made by the customer.</p>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4216.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4216(c)</a> , <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4217.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4217</a> ,<a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=e72799e5ed785e5dcfcc5aa58f33dade&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.8&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> 27 CFR 53.98(a)</a> , and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=e72799e5ed785e5dcfcc5aa58f33dade&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.7.613.14&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.104</a> ]</p>

				<p>Tax-free sales may be made only if the manufacturer, producer, or importer, first purchaser and second purchaser, as the case may be, have registered as required by <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=5498c989bdfc21d80c65f08a7732b645;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.140;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.8.171.8" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.140</a>. <a href="/images/pdfs/forms/f530028.pdf" target="_blank">TTB Form 5300.28</a>, Application for Registration for Tax-Free Transactions Under <a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/4221" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4221</a> must be filed with TTB's National Revenue Center (NRC) in accordance with instructions contained on the reverse of the form. The person who receives an approved Certificate of Registry shall be considered to be registered for purposes of selling or purchasing articles tax-free as provided in <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=5498c989bdfc21d80c65f08a7732b645;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.140;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.8.171.8" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.140</a>.</p>

		<h2 align="center">
			<a id="taxes" name="taxes"></a>Taxes</h2>
		<h2 align="center">Liability</h2>
		<div id="menu79" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub79" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You are liable for FAET if you:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Manufacture or produce an article in the 50 States of the United States or the District of Columbia ; or</li>
					<li>Import an article into the 50 States of the United States or the District of Columbia from a source outside of the United States .</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www4.law.cornell.edu/uscode/26/4181.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">26 U.S.C. 4181</a> and <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=92f451621142f29b1e3d83fd50f0c9d7&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.5.613.1&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.61</a> ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu80" class="mmenu"><strong>To what jurisdictions does FAET apply?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub80" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<table id="table8" style="width: 80%;" border="1" cellspacing="3" cellpadding="3" align="center">
					<tbody>
						<tr bgcolor="#cccccc">
							<th>
								<p align="left"><strong>FAET applies to articles that are: </strong></p>
							</th>
							<td style="width: 245px;">
								<p align="left"><strong>FAET does not apply to articles that are: </strong></p>
							</td>
						</tr>
						<tr valign="top">
							<td style="height: 134px;">
								<p> Manufactured or produced in the 50 States of the United States and the District of Columbia; or</p>
								<p> Imported into the 50 States of the United States and the District of Columbia from a United States possession or a foreign country.</p>
							</td>
							<td>
								<p>Manufactured or produced in a United States possession such as:</p>
								<p>-- The Virgin Islands<br /> -- Puerto Rico<br /> -- Guam<br /> -- American Samoa</p>
							</td>
						</tr>
					</tbody>

			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu81" class="mmenu"><strong>When do I become liable for FAET?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub81" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>You generally become liable for FAET when you transfer title of an article to a purchaser. The moment that you become liable for FAET on an article is often referred to as "attachment of tax."</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu82" class="mmenu"><strong>Where does FAET apply?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub82" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>FAET applies to:</p>
				<p>Portable weapons that use an explosive to expel a projectile such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Pistols</li>
					<li>Revolvers</li>
					<li>Shotguns</li>
					<li>Rifles</li>
					<li>Black powder firearms</li>
					<li>Machine guns</li>
					<li>Antique firearms</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to Revenue Ruling 57-606 and Revenue Ruling 74-137 ]</p>
				<p>Ammunition such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Shells</li>
					<li>Cartridges</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=ecfr&amp;sid=92f451621142f29b1e3d83fd50f0c9d7&amp;rgn=div8&amp;view=text&amp;node=27:1.0.1.3.30.5.613.1&amp;idno=27" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.61(a)(4)</a> ]</p>
				<p>Firearms and ammunition that are unassembled but contain all component parts (such as firearms kits)</p>
				<p>[Refer to Revenue Ruling 56-208 and Revenue Ruling 62-169 ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu83" class="mmenu"><strong>Where does FAET not apply? </strong></div>
		<div id="sub83" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>FAET does not apply to:</p>
				<p>Non-portable weapons (such as cannons)</p>
				<p>Any device that does not use an explosive to expel a projectile such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Air pistols</li>
					<li>Air rifles</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="/images/pdfs/rulings/67-453.html" target="_blank">Revenue Ruling 67-453</a>]</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Starter pistols that use blank ammunition</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="/images/pdfs/rulings/60-233.html" target="_blank">Revenue Ruling 60-233</a>]</p>
				<p>Individual parts of firearms such as <em>:</em></p>
				<ul>
					<li>Frames</li>
					<li>Receivers</li>
					<li>Barrels</li>
					<li>Magazines</li>
					<li>Sights</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=e3984afe7734a5ccdec6be336efed5e6;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.61;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.5.169.1" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.61(b) (5) (ii)</a>]</p>
				<p>Any extra or spare parts that come with a firearm such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Extra barrels</li>
					<li>Extra sights</li>
					<li>Spare magazines</li>
					<li>Spare cylinders</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to <a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=e3984afe7734a5ccdec6be336efed5e6;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.61;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.5.169.1" target="_blank" rel="noopener">27 CFR 53.61(b)(5)(iii) </a>and Revenue Ruling 54-98]</p>
				<p>Accessories such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Cleaning equipment</li>
					<li>Slings</li>
					<li>Slip on recoil pads</li>
					<li>Tools</li>
				</ul>
				<p>[Refer to<a href="http://www.ecfr.gov/cgi/t/text/text-idx?type=simple;c=ecfr;cc=ecfr;sid=e3984afe7734a5ccdec6be336efed5e6;idno=27;region=DIV1;q1=53.61;rgn=div8;view=text;node=27%3A2.0.1.2.6.5.169.1" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> 27 CFR 53.61(b)(5)(iv)</a> and Revenue Ruling 54-98]</p>
				<p>Separate parts of ammunition such as:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Cartridge cases</li>
					<li>Primers</li>
					<li>Bullets</li>
					<li>Powder</li>
				</ul>
				[Refer to <a href="/images/pdfs/rulings/68-463.html" target="_blank">Revenue Ruling 68-463</a>]</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu84" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET if I transfer a firearm classified under the National Firearms Act (NFA)?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub84" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>If you transfer an NFA firearm, you must either:</p>
				<ul>
					<li>Pay the $200.00 NFA transfer tax ($5.00 for any other weapon); or</li>
					<li>Pay FAET on the sale price.</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu85" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET on gifts or donations of articles?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub85" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>This depends on the reason you gave the gift or donation. For example, if you gave or donated an article as a good deed and received no compensation in return, you would probably not be liable for FAET. However, if you gave or donated an article as a promotion or advertisement, you would probably be liable for FAET.</p>
				<strong>Note</strong>: You should contact the NRC for help in determining whether or not you owe FAET on gifts or donations.</div>
		</div>
		<div id="menu86" class="mmenu"><strong>Am I liable for FAET on articles stolen from my business?</strong></div>
		<div id="sub86" class="subMenu">
			<div class="subs">
				<p>No. You are not liable for FAET on any articles stolen from your business (a theft is not considered a sale).</p>
				<p>[Refer to Revenue Ruling 67-58 ]</p>
			</div>
		</div>
